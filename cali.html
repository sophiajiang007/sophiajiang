Quick Facts

Written: February 14, 2025

Genre: Pop

Vibe: Driving down the highway at sunset

Favorite Lyric: "I'll take your California name"

Background

I wrote this on a snowy night after an online Gospel Choir rehearsal. My microphone was muted, so I was able to come up with melodies while everyone else was singing. It was very cold and I was feeling a bit blue so naturally, I was thinking about when I would feel the joy of warmer weather again. I wrote a fun hook earlier in class that day and connected the words and melody during the rehearsal. Then, I ran to the piano that night and started writing the chorus. Then came the bridge, and finally, the verses. I wanted the song to begin as if I was meeting someone for the first time, and “hey, what’s your name?” was the first thing that came to mind. The next morning, I tweaked some lines and the song was done. I was looking for a typical catchy pop vibe with a hint of nostalgia, like something you could play on the radio while cruising down the highway during a summer sunset. That’s why I went for a normal pop structure with lots of repetition. But of course, making sure to have some lyrical and mood nuances between the verses and the prechoruses.

A year later, when I had all the words written for the other songs, I started to produce demos in Ableton. I had practice with software through my classes but it was trial by fire when it came to making my own project. I made everything in my dorm room with a mini MIDI keyboard, a condenser mic, a small Scarlet interface, and mixing headphones—all equipment that I borrowed from my school. I spent a night clicking through all the presets and highlighting sounds that I thought were most fit for what I was looking for. Once I did that, I pretty much started with the first thing I knew how to do, which was play the chords in MIDI, then record my vocals. Then the basic drum pattern came to me when I played it all back, and I recorded it in. I wanted it to have a sense of movement as if the beat was running from side to side, so I panned it left to right with the automation. For my next sound, I chose a pluck guitar and changed the stiffness setting to add texture. Next, and probably my favorite part, was adding background vocals. I wanted them to be a lower under-hum that would lift up the lead vocal. Finally, my mixing professor, Sunny, who also kindly masters my demos for me, suggested I add another element, like a bass-line. This didn’t come intuitively to me and I was crunched for time so I explored another bright guitar and used EQ to cut out certain frequencies and bring out others in order to get some radiant sounds. This was also a good way to differentiate the sounds of the same guitar preset between different sections of the song. Once everything was recorded, I mixed it, which basically meant I had it on loop and kept adjusting the gain to even out the levels, as well as adding effects.
